Atira-Class Asteroids

Also known as Apoheles, Atiras are a highly exclusive group of Near-Earth Asteroids whose orbits are contained entirely within the orbit of Earth. Their furthest point from the Sun (aphelion) is strictly less than Earth's closest point to the Sun (perihelion).

The Inner System Challenge

Hiding in the Glare

Because they are closer to the Sun than Earth, Atiras only appear in our sky during the day or at twilight. This makes them incredibly difficult for ground-based telescopes to discover and track.

Intense Solar Energy

Mining operations on an Atira would have access to abundant, continuous solar power. This makes them prime candidates for Optical Mining techniques that rely on concentrated sunlight.

High Delta-V Cost

Counter-intuitively, it takes a massive amount of energy (Delta-V) to "drop down" toward the Sun. Spacecraft must shed a lot of Earth's orbital velocity to reach an Atira, making early missions expensive.

Notable Atira Candidates

As of recent surveys, only a few dozen Atiras have been officially confirmed. Here are the most notable targets for future study:

Designation Diameter (Est.) Orbital Period Mining Interest
163693 Atira ~4.8 km 233 days The namesake of the class. It is a binary system, making it an excellent target for studying asteroid composition and gravity.
434326 (2004 JG6) ~0.5 km 185 days Has an incredibly short orbital period. Passes close to Venus and Mercury, offering complex but intriguing trajectory options.
594913 'Ayló'chaxnim ~2.0 km 151 days The first discovered Vatira (an asteroid entirely inside the orbit of Venus). Extremely difficult to reach, but bathed in solar energy.
